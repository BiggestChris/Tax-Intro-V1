Employee

Ok, looks like you're going to be an employee! There's a bunch of legal definitions around what it means to be an employee, but we don't actually need to go through that right now. The important bit is that when your employer pays you each month they will give you a Payslip. And your employer will also automatically deduct tax from your earnings and pay it to HMRC on your behalf. This massively simplifies the process and the risk of you paying the wrong tax is significantly reduced!

It's still worth understanding how this works, and importantly if you have any other sources of income, or change jobs or decide to work for yourself during the year it's important to understand how the tax you've paid so far is calculated.

What's on my payslip?

This is the total amount you are being paid by your employer - this is the same thing as your salary (albeit split up by month). This is the number they likely quoted to you when you accepted the job! However, the important bit is that this isn't actually the number that hits your bank account.

This is the amount of income tax you need to pay. The important bit to remember is that this is calculated based upon your current income extrapolated out for the entire year. This means that if working for 12 months means you would pay tax at a different rate, but you end up only working for 6 months, then the tax is taken out now at the higher rate.

This is the amount of National Insurance contributions you have to pay as an employee. You don't need to know the small differences between NICs and Tax, but the simplest way to look at it is that it is like an extra tax you pay on top of income tax. So whilst base rate income tax is 20%, the base rate NICs is 12%, so the lowest rate of actual tax you're paying is closer to 32% rather than 20% when you think about your employment income.

Do you have a student loan? Then this is where this is paid off! The amount you pay will be dependent upon your type of loan (size/when you took it out/which plan you took). This is taken out of your payslip here, but please bear in mind it is the Student Loans Company rather than HMRC than manage your total balance.

This one can be a bit of a surprise, but some benefits that your employer gives you for free, you actually need to pay tax on! This is in place to stop your employer effectively paying you in food/gifts/cars etc. rather than cash to avoid you paying tax! The likely tax benefits will be medical insurance provided by your employer, and a company car if you have that. However, it will depend upon the employee benefits your employer offers and what the relevant treatment is for that.

And then of course, there are any pension contributions you make. The important things with pension contributions is that these come out of your pre-tax income or your Gross pay as we talked about earlier. So one of the key advantages of saving into your pension is that you actually wind up putting more of your money in, than if you take it out of your post-tax income (where you have paid tax) and put it in a savings account.

And then at the bottom you'll have this, effectively your Gross pay, minus all of the other points above. This will be the actual number that hits your bank account.
